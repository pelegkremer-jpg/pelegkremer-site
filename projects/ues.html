Urban Structure and Environmental Conditions

The Structure of Urban Landscapes (STURLA) classification, from Berlin–NYC case studies to a global analysis.

Program of work

2014 to present

NSF support

2018 to 2024

My role

PI on NSF GSS award; lead methodologist

Key collaborators

Dagmar Haase (Humboldt), Neele Larondelle, Zoe Hamstead, Timon McPhearson, Kabindra Shakya (Villanova Chemistry)

The question

How does the three-dimensional structure of cities, the way buildings, vegetation, and pavement combine at the block scale, shape the heat, air quality, and ecological processes that residents actually experience? Most urban environmental analyses rely on flat, two-dimensional land cover and miss this vertical dimension.

What we do

At the heart of this work is the Structure of Urban Landscapes (STURLA) classification, which captures common three-dimensional compositions of urban form and links them to environmental indicators. The framework was first developed in a Berlin–New York City comparison (Larondelle et al. 2014), refined for New York City (Hamstead et al. 2016), and extended to Philadelphia (Mitz et al. 2021). In parallel, with Dr. Kabindra Shakya in Villanova Chemistry, we built a mobile platform for fine-scale air pollution monitoring (Shakya et al. 2019).

Current work pushes STURLA in several directions:

  • From surface temperature to the air temperature people experience. Madeline Scolio led work linking remotely sensed surface temperature to air temperature, opening a path to use globally available remote sensing for urban heat exposure assessment (Scolio et al. 2024, Urban Climate).
  • Grid sensitivity. Dennis Weaver and Justin Stewart tested 399 grid configurations and found that grid size, not orientation, drives results, with the tightest urban structure-function relationships at roughly 200 to 500 meters (Kremer, Weaver, Stewart 2026, Ecological Informatics). The finding has implications well beyond STURLA.
  • Global scale. A current single-author paper applies STURLA via Google Earth Engine to 11,214 urban areas across 178 countries, testing whether case-study temperature patterns generalize worldwide and surfacing climate-dependent cooling effects of trees and water (Kremer, in review, Urban Climate).
